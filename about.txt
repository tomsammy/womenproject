Beyond 16 days of activism in the Fight against Gender Based Violence in Nigeria

Ijeoma Eteng EmeribeIjeoma Eteng Emeribe
Ijeoma Eteng Emeribe
Published Dec 13, 2021
+ Follow
Globally, since 25th November 2021 till date the Gender Based Violence and women's right has been the big issues trending over the media, especially as we live in a world that continuously provides mechanisms to trample on those rights that seeks to defend women from all forms of discrimination, violence, abuse and injustice. Gender based violence is a global health issue that cuts across boundaries of economic wealth, culture, religion, age, and sexual orientation.

 As we have just rounded up the campaign on the 16 Days of Activism Against Gender-Based Violence, the question is what next? How do we ensure that this activism goes beyond a 16 days campaign to a 365days campaign? How do we translate 16 days effort to a strategic sustainable adopted component that will drive the adoption and creation of policy frameworks around all the states?
The 16 Days of Activism against Gender-Based Violence is an annual international campaign that kicks off on 25 November, the International Day for the Elimination of Violence against Women, and runs until 10 December, Human Rights Day. It was started by activists at the inaugural Women’s Global Leadership Institute in 1991 and continues to be coordinated each year by the Center for Women’s Global Leadership2.

16 Days of Activism against Gender-Based Violence is used as an advocacy strategy by state and non-state actors, demand and supply side, development partners around the world to call for the prevention and elimination of violence against women and girls. The Campaign tremendously became an efficient and effective tool to raise public awareness and consciousness, not only about violence against women and girls, but also about the United Nations Human Rights System in general.

GBV is fueled largely by inequitable gender norms, it can affect people at different points in their lives and ranges from intimate partner violence to sexual coercion and abuse to child marriage. Each of these can have physical, sexual, medical or emotional dimensions, it is important to ensure that conversations around the disparity between the male and female gender, and the cultural and religious practices that are vile to natural justice, equity, and good conscience, are not allowed to stop.

Gender-based violence against women has been in existence since time memorial, but we have gotten to a point where these issues can no longer be seen as a norm (al) part of living that requires no attention or as one of those gender issues but as a human right and development issue that needs a sustainable and coordinated intervention and international attention.

Issues in Focus

A report by the World Health Organization (WHO) focused on the largest study of the prevalence of violence against women. The report was on behalf of a special working group of the United Nations, and it revealed that across their lifetime, one in three women are subjected to physical or sexual violence by an intimate partner or sexual violence from a non-partner. Given the high levels of stigma and under-reporting of sexual abuse, the true figure may be significantly higher 1(Nigeria health watch) other forms of abuse and discrimination on women has also continued all over the country sadly submerged in a culture of absolute silence with no tools or laws for civil society to effectively engage in driving intervention that will salvage women who are currently going through GBV, the ethical question that we have kept asking everyone is the reason behind the lack of will power by the state governments to domesticate the convention on the elimination of all forms of discrimination against women (CEDAW) which will empower stakeholders to engage on the fight against GBV and get sustainable and effective result.

The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW), adopted in 1979 by the UN General Assembly, is often described as an international bill of rights for women. Consisting of a preamble and 30 articles, it defines what constitutes discrimination against women and sets up an agenda for national action to end such discrimination.

Nigeria’s Federal Government ratified CEDAW in 1985 without reservation. The government signed the Optional Protocol to the Convention in 2000 and ratified it in 2004 As a State Party, Nigeria is obliged to implement the Convention by using all appropriate means to eliminate all forms of discrimination against women

It becomes evidently sad that despite the laudable provisions of the Convention on the Elimination of all forms of Discrimination against Women, (CEDAW), the Nigerian Government has been slow in developing frameworks for the domestication of the CEDAW.

Translating the 16days of activism to 365 days will mean the following

·        Inclusive Economic Empowerment of Women

A National strategic plan to improve the economic life of the Nigerian women should be developed to empower women, this is aimed at creating an economically independent Nigerian woman, studies have shown that economic empowerment of women is key in reducing Gender Based Violent as more of the victims depends entirely on their oppressor and victimizer, this strategy is expected to be inclusive to ensure that women with disabilities are not left behind in the process.  
·        Strengthening Institutional Efforts to Address GBV 

To strengthen the institution’s efforts to prevent and respond to risks of GBV, and particularly sexual exploitation and abuse that may arise in our diverse communities, efforts should be made to increase funding for community-based organization to respond to not just cases of occurrences of gender-based issues but to train community-based women leaders to identify potential case of abuse and roll out advocacy to change local norm in the community by engaging the gate keepers.

Safe spaces need to be provided for venting and a platform to discuss issues of Gender and Sexual Based Violence with the aim of getting the needed support.

Civil societies working in the rural communities where most of this case occurs in greater percentage but remains unreported should be aided to initiate interventions that will increase the local reportage of gender-based violence activities within the communities.

·        Medical approach to halting gender-based violence

The state and non-state actor’s needs to set a sustainable partnership that will ensure access to medical attention and care for victims and survivors of gender-based violence in all states and down to the local communities of Nigeria where it mostly needed and unreported.

·        Accessible, Inclusive and improved Justice System

While pervasive, gender-based violence is not inevitable. It can be prevented and managed adequately if the Justice system is engaged to effectively handle GBV related cases, introducing more sophisticated forensic equipment in investigating GBV related cases and most importantly training the law enforcement officer in handling reporting GBV cases.

How Woman Africa foundation is fighting GBV

In ensuring that the woman Africa foundation translates the 16 days of activism to 365 days of activism and commitment in sustaining the fight against GBV , our organization is in the process of  rolling out a platform, a SAFE space to address the issue of culture of silence on the reportage of Gender Based Violence ,this platform will ensure survivors and victims’ discuss issues of Gender and Sexual Based Violence with the aim of getting the needed support to live a productive life and move beyond the psychosocial trauma they may have experienced as a result of any form of abuse.

This SAFE space will be located in Abuja with a call-in line to engage participants who are not within Abuja.
We can stop Gender Based Violence in Nigeria if we all play our parts, our collective multi sectional effort will help translate our interventions to success, with this we can bring to zero level the incidence of GBV.
